The Hour Makes
the Crime

Twenty-two years ago, San Francisco’s billboards sold flip phones and dot-com dreams. Today, they sell Artificial Intelligence. But while the tech products have evolved, the city’s criminal patterns remain strikingly consistent. By mapping two decades of incident reports, we can see how the hour of the day still acts as a prominent predictor from white-collar fraud to street-level assault.

About the data

This analysis draws on the San Francisco Police Department (SFPD) Incident Reports database, a public record of crimes reported to the SFPD from 2003 to the present. Each entry logs the type of crime, the date and time it was reported, and the neighborhood in which it occurred. Note that the data is a result of a concatenation of two datasets: one from 2003 to 2018 and one from 2018 to the present.

We focus on nine crime categories: Arson, Assault, Burglary, Fraud, Larceny/Theft, Missing Persons, Motor Vehicle Theft, Prostitution, and Robbery, collectively known in the course as Focus Crimes. These were selected because they represent a meaningful cross-section of criminal activity and are just our personal favourites.

To make two decades of data align with each other, we unified disparate reporting styles into one modern system. Sometimes that meant making executive calls, like deciding "Vandalism" and "Malicious Mischief" are basically related. Keep in mind, though, that this is a map of reported incidents. If a crime wasn't called in or an officer didn't file a report, it’s not in our charts. We’re looking at the city through the lens of police paperwork, which is often its own version of reality. And as for the timing? It’s accurate enough to gather insights, but just messy enough that you shouldn't bet your life on it.

Every Crime Has Its Peak Hour

Two decades of San Francisco crime data shows us that each crime has its favourite part of the day (at least most of them). We grouped the incidents into our “focus crimes” and plotted their distribution across a 24-hour cycle. The results? Both interesting, but also a bit concerning. While most of the city sleeping and charging their batteries, arsonists and robbers are just clocking into their shift. Actually, the evening/nightly pattern is very common. In fact, if you’re looking for one takeaway, it’s this: according to the data, 7 p.m. is officially the worst time to go out.

"Every crime follows its own internal logic." — from the data

All jokes aside, every crime follows its own internal logic. A missing person is usually reported throughout the day. Prostitution, on the other hand, is a classic "5-to-9" operation. And if you’re wondering why we haven't mentioned fraud yet? Don’t worry, we’ll come back to that.

Before we show you the data, a quick reality check: these incidents are logged by police officers manually typing into a database. Are we all sometimes lazy? Yes. Therefore, you shouldn't treat this data as the single source of truth down to the minute. Now, let’s talk about the elephant in the room: fraud. Fraud doesn't just happen "out of the blue"; it’s usually the culmination of a long investigation. While the charts might suggest a tidy schedule, ask yourself: is that a genuine crime pattern, or just a bunch of "placeholder" timestamps from officers who didn't want to guess when the embezzlement actually started? As said, the data is never perfect.

Figure 1 — Time-of-Day Distribution by Crime Type

Each violin shows the distribution of reported incidents across the 24-hour day for a given crime type, based on SFPD data from 2003–2025. The width at any hour reflects how frequently crimes of that type are reported at that time. Dotted lines mark the 25th, 50th, and 75th percentiles. Notice how for some crimes, e.g. larceny theft, the crime peaks before 8 p.m. while for others, e.g. prostitution, things do not get going until after 10 p.m.

The crime of the hour

But not all crimes are created equal, and some are clearly more prevalent then others at certain time of the day. By visualizing the probability of each crime type being reported at different hours of the day with a heatmap, we can get a clearer picture of the crime patterns in San Francisco the last two decades. And although the heatmap also clealy follows the same peak pattern as the violin plot, with most crimes being more likely to be reported in the evening/night hours. What stands out is just how much of a difference there is in the probability of each of these crimes being reported.

The heatmap clearly visualize how the probability of both Arson and Prostitution is very low at any hour of the day, with the probabilty of arson being reported never going above 0.02 at any time during the day and the probability of prostitution being reported never going above 0.04. Meanwhile, larceny theft is the clear "winner", not only is it the crime of the hour but rather the crime of every hour, being the crime with the highest probability of being reported at any hour of the day. Turns out, if there is any crime that you should be on the guard for, it's not the one that happens at night, but the one that happens at all hours of the day.

Figure 2 — Probability of Each Crime Type by Hour

The probability is calculated from the data that has been recorded and kept by the San Francisco Police Department for the last two decades. Another discovery that sticks out in the heatmap, our theory of officers just picking a random time for fraud reports, might not be so far-fetched after all. As the heatmap somehow shows that the probability of a fraud being reported is highest at midnight. Not exactly the crime that we think of as a "midnight crime". But again, this is where the data's limitations come into play, and especially when it is reported by people who might have other potenially more important things to do than filling out a report.

Has the Pattern Changed Over Time?

While we've established that crimes follow daily rhythms, an interesting question arises: has this pattern remained steady over the past two decades? As the city has evolved one might expect the timing of crimes to shift as well.

To investigate this, we plotted the probability of certain categories of crime occurring at each hour of the day, using our first year of data as a baseline. We then tracked the difference between this baseline and each subsequent year. While the overall pattern is relatively steady, specific categories display shifts in the city's heartbeat.

Figure 3 — Hourly Crime Probability Changes Over Time

Blue bars represent the baseline probability from 2003. Use the slider to track changes by selecting a new year to compare to, and the dropdown to select a crime category. Red bars indicate increases and Green bars indicate decreases relative to that baseline.

The data shows a divide between violent crime and property offenses. Violent crimes like assault and robbery have remained relatively consistent, their hourly patterns remaining essentially unchanged over twenty years. On the other hand property crimes, such as arson, burglary and larceny show higher sensitivity to the city's changing landscape as time progresses, shifting more visibly from the baseline. Prostitution has moved later into the night, moving from an early evening window to the late-night window. Fraud is an outlier in how it has changed here, being a non-violent crime, that nevertheless has remained relatively consistent. Though a spike at midnight is seen. This is likely an artifact of the dataset and not a reflection of when fraud actually occurs.

So while the timing of violence remains relatively fixed, other crimes seem to be better matching the city’s evolving nature.
